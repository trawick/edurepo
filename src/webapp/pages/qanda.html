Questions and Answers

Who are you, and why are you doing this?

My name is Jeff Trawick and I am a software developer located in Raleigh, North Carolina. I believe that this application of relatively common technology and the infusion of open software, open information culture can greatly improve the effectiveness of existing efforts to develop and share course materials and communicate current lessons to parents, especially at the middle school and high school levels, when parents find it increasingly difficult to understand how they can contribute to their children's understanding of current activities.

Can I use this?

Yes, of course. Download the software from https://github.com/trawick/edurepo and deploy the software on your server, define your courses, load databases with resources applicable to your learning objectives, and so forth.

No, really...

Unfortunately, you're correct; it isn't feasible for most people to do that. You need someone else to maintain the computer resources that make it work, with minimal effort on your part.

As long as your course is generally useful and you or someone else is willing to publish formal course descriptions and possibly additional materials for the learning objectives of the course, you will be able to use a publicly-accessible server for your class.

If your course contains private materials or isn't generally useful to others, you'll need to run your own server or pay a vendor to provide that as a service.

Most K-12 and college courses are examples of courses that will find a home in a freely available, publicly accessible server.

Religious instruction courses and courses containing proprietary information are but two examples of courses that would require the use of a privately funded server. (But the software itself is freely available for any purpose.)

How do I know the servers will handle the load if I commit to using this?

It is no secret that web based software does not always support the number of people that want to use it. Unfortunately, edjective.org does not currently have available the limitless engineering and server resources to handle any possible demand. We will have to grow with demand and with the ability to pay for resources.

A school system can mitigate any risk of server availability by setting up dedicated servers for the use of their students, largely avoiding the unpredictable impact of the use of public systems by unknown numbers of students across the world. The school system can control the use of its servers by limiting the number of classes using it to match the server resources they are able to allocate.

Over time the public server will evolve to predictably, reliably support a very large number of users, just like other resources we use daily on the internet.

What happens if Jeff quits working on this?

The software and educational materials are freely licensed. The educational materials are maintained in machine readable format suitable for use even without using the software developed for this project.

In order to ensure that this project can survive independent of any particular individuals, I will promote the following:

  • The software components should be maintained as a largely independent project which is part of an existing non-profit organization with the cultural, technical, legal, and financial ability to oversee ongoing development and maintenance by interested parties (something like the Apache Software Foundation).
  • The educational materials themselves should be maintained separately by a new or existing non-profit organization that can provide the appropriate culture and other resources suitable for educational materials and the community which maintain them; this may be very different than an open source software community. This same organization should operate the definitive servers which make the materials freely accessible to as many people as possible (something like the Wikimedia Foundation).
  • Even though it is crucial to provide the core services without cost to anyone, the ability of individuals and vendors to profit from involvement in the ecosystem must be maintained; this provides a powerful incentive to contribute directly to the projects as well as to provide new types of end user software that can provide a better experience for teachers, parents, and students.
