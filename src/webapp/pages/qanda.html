Questions and Answers

Who are you, and why are you doing this?

My name is Jeff Trawick and I am a software developer located in Raleigh, North Carolina. I believe that the application of relatively simple technology and open software culture can improve the effectiveness of existing efforts on the part of educators, and that any such improvement is valuable to society.

Can I use this?

Yes, of course. Download the software from https://github.com/trawick/edurepo and deploy the software on your server, define your courses, load databases with resources applicable to your learning objectives, and so forth.

No, really...

Unfortunately, you're correct; it isn't feasible for most people to do that. You need someone else to maintain the computer resources that make it work, with minimal effort on your part.

As long as your course is generally useful and you or someone else is willing to publish formal course descriptions and possibly additional materials for the learning objectives of the course, you will be able to use a publicly-accessible server for your class.

If your course contains private materials or isn't generally useful to others, you'll need to run your own server or pay a vendor to provide that as a service.

Most K-12 and college courses are examples of courses that will find a home in a freely available, publicly accessible server.

Religious instruction courses and courses containing proprietary information are examples of courses that would require the use of a different server.

I need a service level agreement, or at least to see it work reliably for a long time, before committing to using this

It is no secret that web based software may not always support the number of people that want to use it. Unfortunately, this is not an operation that can afford to make available limitless engineering or server resources to handle any possible demand. We will have to grow with demand and with the ability to pay for resources.

Fortunately, a school system has the ability to provide dedicated servers for the use of their students, largely avoiding the unpredictable impact of the use of public systems by unknown numbers of students across the world. The school system can control the use of the servers (by limiting the number of classes) to match the server resources they are able to allocate to it. Please contact me for more information about this.

Over time the public server will evolve to predictably, reliably support a very large number of users.

What happens if Jeff quits working on this?

The software and educational materials are and will be freely licensed. The educational materials will be stored in machine readable format even without using this particular software.

In order to ensure that this project can survive independent of any particular individuals, I will promote the following:

  • The software components should be maintained as a largely independent project which is part of an existing non-profit organization with the cultural, technical, legal, and financial ability to support ongoing development and maintenance by interested parties. (something like the Apache Software Foundation)
  • The educational materials themselves should be maintained separately by a new or existing non-profit organization that can provide the appropriate culture and other resources. This same organization should operate the definitive servers which make the materials readily accessible to as many people as possible. (something like the Wikimedia Foundation)
  • Even though it is crucial to provide the core services without cost to anyone who can make use of it, the ability of individuals and vendors to profit from involvement in the ecosystem should be respected, with the profit incentive seen as an important aspect of long term survival.
